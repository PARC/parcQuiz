Tasmanian Devil
Vigorous lone animals that resist change. T-Devils often have no clear vision for innovation.
  • Current business is often sustained by powerful brand equity left over from past successes.
  • This legacy – and the inflexible mental models it reflects – can inhibit growth through innovation.
  • T-Devils struggle to stay relevant as markets change; growth is often delayed, then expensive (e.g., acquisitions).
  • All energy goes into driving the current business. Management does not encourage or reward innovation efforts.
Devil Recommendations
  1. First, decide whether innovation is needed everywhere or just in some business areas/units. If the challenge is in specific areas, go straight to Step 4; if the need is companywide, go to Step 2.
  2. Ensure senior leaders are committed to organic growth. If leaders are content with current performance, they will have no interest in innovation.
  3. Frame a clear strategic roadmap that commits the company to developing valuable offerings in growth markets. Be careful to focus: a narrower, distinctive growth ambition will liberate more energy than an expansive but generic one! The roadmap sets priorities for innovation projects.
  4. Learn by doing: set up innovation teams, give them a process and a mandate, and get some projects going in areas where the company really needs to upgrade it’s offerings. Be generous with these early efforts: you are learning the method as well as harvesting the growth opportunities.
  5. Extend and expand: as results come in, celebrate successful initiatives and be open about experiments that didn’t succeed. Tweak methods and export the model to other areas.
